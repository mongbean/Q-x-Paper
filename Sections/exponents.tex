\begin{lemma}
    $a^na^m = a^{n+m} \quad \forall a \in \bZ,\, n, m \in \bZ \cup \{0\}$
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Let $S= \left \{ n \in \bZ^+ \mid \exists m \in \bZ^+ \cup \{0\} \text{ s.t. } a^na^m \neq a^{n+m}\right \}$. For the sake of contradiction, S is not empty. By the well-ordering principle, since $S\subset \bZ^+$, there must be a least element $l\in S$. First, $\forall m \in \bZ^+$, 
    \begin{align*}
        a \cdot a^{m-1} &= a^m \\
        a^1 \cdot a^{m-1} &= a^m 
    \end{align*}
    Thus, $1 \notin S$ as $m-1 \in \bZ^+$. Therefore, by NIBZO $l>1$ and $(l-1) \in \bZ^+$, but $l-1 \notin S$.
    \begin{align*}
        a^{l-1} a^m &= a^{l+m-1}\\
        a \m a^{l-1} a^m &= a \m a^{l+m-1}\\
        a^la^m&=a^{l+m}
    \end{align*}
    Thus, $l \notin S$. However, this contradicts that $l$ is the least element of $S$. So, $S$ must be empty.
\end{proof}
